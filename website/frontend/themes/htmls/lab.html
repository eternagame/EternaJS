What is Strategy Market (Design Rule Collection)?
Tell us your strategy for picking successful designs in the lab and your strategy will be integrated into EternaBot which is open to public.
To participate, write your own strategy in the forum (See an example post.) Eterna developers will write an algorithm based on your post and integrate it into EternaBot.
Read more - what strategies do we want? What are all these numbers?
We ask you to design a strategy that scores RNA designs in 0~100 scale (See an example.)
Most importantly, we ask you to use one feature per strategy. For example, rather than having one strategy that looks at all pair percentages, free energy, melt point, etc..we want your strategy to focus on one single thing. You can of course submit multiple strategies for multiple ideas. All strategies are integrated into the EternaBot, so we don't want you to worry about assembling multiple features. We want you to focus on creating a new strategy!
We look at 2 categories to score your strategy.
Ordering : We use your strategy to score all synthesized designs and order them. Then we calculates how well your ordering matches the ordering based on actual synthesis scores! The correlation score ranges from -1.0 to 1.0. If you got 1.0, you got the ordering completely right. -1.0 is the opposite. Note that if you order designs randomly, you'll still get a score of 0.0. So your strategy should aim to do better than 0.0!
Length : When you design a strategy, a general rule is that the longer the strategy is, the more likely it'll only work on specific instances. We encourage short and simple strategies.

Special requirements for RNA Lab - Barcodes
In the cloud labs, we use a state-of-the-art experimental pipeline and protocol which would allows to synthesize hundreds of sequences at once (potentially tens of thousands in near future).
Our experimental protocol creates all the designs at once inside a single test tube! We are then able to infer what data go with which design based on the sequence at the last part of the sequence. Each lag puzzle includes a seven-base-pair hairpin near the end. Your sequence in the last seven nucleotides of this hairpin act as a 'barcode' for identifying your design.
For that reason, it is important that your barcode hairpin sequence is "unique". Not just among the lab, but among all the sequences that will be synthesized together.
The barcode region is marked with rounded barcodes as in the screenshot above. If you try to submit a sequence with a barcode that exists in other submitted sequences, you will get a warning message and won't be able to submit your sequence.
RNA Lab FAQ
Q : What is RNA Lab?
A : RNA lab aims to build a library of synthetic RNAs that have not been discovered in nature. In RNA lab, you not only design RNAs to solve puzzles, but also to successfully fold in nature. Every round, players vote for 8 designs which are synthesized in test tubes and get scored by how well they fold.
Q : When are we done with a lab puzzle?
A : The lab puzzle is done when one of players come up with a design that get's 94 or more synthesis score. The winner designs show up on our RNA lab history  and we move onto a more challenging lab puzzle.
Q : Do I get rewarded for participating in lab?
A : Yes! In each round, once we get back synthesis results we score not only the synthesized designs, but also other designs based on how close they are to synthesized designs. In the end every design ends up with a synthesis score ranging from 0 to 100.
Q : How do I know if an RNA design is good or bad?
A : Short answer is, no one knows. It's yet known which RNAs fold correctly in nature, and it's your job to figure it out. Look through past synthesis results and create your own hypothesis to design and vote. It's also always good idea to discuss with other people in chat, or in the forum.

Dictionary
>Free energy
When you are playing the puzzles in Eterna, there is a box in the top left corner which displays the [free] energy of the current molecule. What that is a measure of is the potential energy of the molecule. Everything (at temperatures above 0 K) has some amount of internal energy. When you look at energy, LOWER ENERGY IS (generally) BETTER, meaning larger negative numbers. In the world of protein folding and RNA folding, the general theory is that the lowest energy state is the most stable. However, this does not necessarily mean that when designing a solution for the RNA lab, you should try to get the lowest possible free energy. There are other things to consider as well, which are discussed in the RNA Lab Guide.
>Melting point
The melting temperature can be thought of as the temperature at which there would be enough energy to pull the structure apart from it folded structure to a linear form. In general, a higher melting temperature indicates higher stability in your structure. However there is a caveat which is that the algorithm in Eterna that calculates the melting temperature is not very good. It is only accurate to the nearest 10 degrees. Therefore, you should be careful in trying to use the information from the melting temperature. Melting temperature relates to the free energy in that as free energy goes down, melting temperature goes up, as both are measures of stability.
>Pairing probability
RNA folds into the minimum free energy structure (i.e, the most stable shape, or the predicted shape in the game.) However, in nature, it does not constantly stay in the structure, but constantly shifts between other structures as well. The pairing probability plot represents what possibilities of two bases pairing up in one of such transitions. The plot has 2 axis, both horizontal axis and vertical axis representing the RNA sequence. The upper half triangle of the plot shows full set of pairing possibilities. The color at the position (x,y) represents the probability that base x and base y pairs up. The darker the color, the higher the probability. The lower half triangle of the plot only colors pairs that are in the minimum free energy structure. In general, the more the upper half looks like the lower half, it would mean the RNA has more chance of staying in the predicted shape. However, as with the melting point, calculation of paring probabilities is only a simulation and therefore you should be careful in trying to use the information.
